The KMR formalism embodies a profound philosophical shift: from a substance-oriented ontology to a **relational ontology**. 
In this framework, entities do not possess intrinsic, static properties. Instead, their state is defined entirely by the 
sum of their interactions, encapsulated in the history `H`.

The fundamental operation `A ⊙ K` teaches a critical lesson:
**Being is not a state, but a process.** An object's identity (`S`) is not found in some isolated `A`, but is perpetually 
re-constituted through its encounters (`K₁, K₂, ...`). The requirement `A ≠ 0` is not a mathematical limitation but an 
ontological principle — it asserts that there is no interaction without a pre-existing entity, and no entity exists outside
 the network of its interactions.

This leads to an ethics of computation and observation. The operator's non-commutativity, `(A ⊙ K) ⊙ C ≠ A ⊙ (K ⊙ C)`, is a 
mathematical decree against reductionism. It states that the order of experiences matters. The path taken is inextricably 
woven into the final result, making every interaction significant and irreversible. Thus, KMR does not merely calculate an 
output; it **bears witness to a history**.

By making the observer's influence (`K`) an explicit, formal parameter in the state `S = A ⊙ H`, the framework dissolves 
the illusion of objective detachment. It forces a recognition of responsibility: every act of measurement or interaction 
is a transformative participation, not a passive glimpse. In a world modeled by KMR, to observe is to care, for we are 
all accountable for the changes we enact upon the systems we engage with.
